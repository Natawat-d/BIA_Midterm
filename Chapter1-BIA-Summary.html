3. Decision Support Systems (DSS)

Definition

DSS = interactive computer-based systems that help decision makers utilize data and models to solve unstructured problems. (Gorry & Scott-Morton, 1971)
They couple the intellectual resources of individuals with the capabilities of the computer to improve the quality of decisions. "Decision support" is an umbrella term, and BI is an evolution of these concepts over time.

Simon's Decision-Making Process (1977)

1. Intelligence→ 2. Design→ 3. Choice→ 4. Implementation→ (5. Monitoring)
PhaseWhat happens
IntelligenceDefine the problem or opportunity.
DesignConstruct a model that describes the real-world problem and identify possible solutions.
ChoiceCompare, choose, and recommend a potential solution.
ImplementationPut the recommended solution to work.
Monitoring(Often treated as part of intelligence) — track results.

Classic illustration: the decision to purchase a car walks through all five phases.

Early Decision Support Framework (Gorry & Scott-Morton, 1971)

Decisions are classified along two axes — type of decision × type of control:

Operational Control Managerial Control Strategic Planning
Structured Accounts receivable/payable, order entry Budget analysis, short-term forecasting, make-or-buy Financial management, investment portfolio, warehouse location
Semistructured Production scheduling, inventory control Credit evaluation, budget prep, project scheduling Building a plant, mergers & acquisitions, new-product planning
Unstructured Buying software, approving loans, help desk Negotiating, recruiting an executive, buying hardware R&D planning, new tech development, social responsibility

Components of a DSS

  • Data management — internal/external data (ERP/POS, legacy, web)
  • Model management — and external models
  • Knowledge-based subsystems — tied to the organizational knowledgebase
  • User interface — connecting the manager (user) to the system

4. Business Intelligence (BI)

Definition

Broad definition: BI is an umbrella term that combines architectures, tools, databases, analytical tools, applications, and methodologies. It is a content-free expression, so it means different things to different people.
  • BI's major objective: enable easy access to data (and models) so business managers can conduct analysis.
  • BI helps transform data into the action chain below:
Data→ Information / Knowledge→ Decisions→ Action

A Brief History of BI

The term "BI" was coined by the Gartner Group in the mid-1990s, but the concept is much older:

1970s — MIS reporting: static / periodic reports, DSS.
1980s — Executive Information Systems (EIS).
1990s — OLAP; dynamic, multidimensional, ad-hoc reporting → the term "BI" is coined.
2010s — AI & data/text mining; web portals/dashboards, Big Data, social media, analytics.
2020s — "Yet to be seen."

Architecture of BI — Four Major Components

ComponentRole
Data warehouseHolds the source data, organized/summarized/standardized by technical staff.
Business analyticsA collection of tools for manipulating, mining, and analyzing the data in the warehouse.
Performance & strategyBusiness performance management (BPM) for monitoring and analyzing performance.
User interfacee.g., dashboards used by managers/executives.
(Future)An emerging intelligent component.

Origins & drivers (Fig. 1.9) feeding into BI include querying/reporting, ETL, metadata, data marts, EIS/ESS, financial reporting, OLAP, dashboards & scorecards, workflow, alerts, data & text mining, predictive analytics, broadcasting tools, portals, and spreadsheets.

Planning, Alignment & Real-Time BI

  • Planning & execution must align across Business, Organization, Functionality, and Infrastructure.
  • A BI Competency Center links BI to strategy, encourages user–IS interaction, and acts as a repository of best practices.
  • Real-time / on-demand BI is enabled by RFID, web services, and intelligent agents — but justify whether real-time is worth the extra expense.

6. Types of Analytics

Per INFORMS, analytics is the combination of computer technology, management science techniques, and statistics to solve real problems — the process of developing actionable decisions or recommendations based on insights from historical data.

Descriptive

"What happened? What is happening?"
Enablers
  • Business reporting
  • Dashboards & scorecards
  • Data warehousing / OLAP
  • Descriptive statistics & visualization
Outcome: well-defined problems & opportunities (retrospective analysis of historic data).

Predictive

"What will happen? Why will it happen?"
Enablers
  • Data mining
  • Text mining / web mining
  • Forecasting (time series)
Outcome: accurate projections of future events — using past data to predict the future.

Prescriptive

"What should I do? Why should I do it?"
Enablers
  • Optimization
  • Simulation
  • Multi-criteria decision modeling
  • Heuristic programming / expert systems
Outcome: best possible decisions & actions — uses descriptive + predictive to build and choose among alternatives.

Analytics Examples in Selected Domains

Healthcare (Humana): preventing falls in a senior population; predictive models to identify the highest-risk membership in a health insurer.

Retail value chain (Table 1.1):

Analytic ApplicationBusiness QuestionBusiness Value
Inventory Optimization Which products have high demand? Which are slow-moving/obsolete? Forecast fast movers to avoid stock-outs; turn over slow movers by bundling with high-demand items.
Price Elasticity What net margin do I have? How much discount can I give? Optimize markdown prices and bundle pricing to protect margin dollars.
Market Basket Analysis What products should I bundle? Same or different categories? Affinity analysis reveals hidden correlations to drive cross-sell / up-sell.

10. Analytics in Action — Purina PANDA Platform

"How Big Will Be the Beef? Understanding Animals' Eating Behavior and Their Final Weight."

This is the chapter's one fully worked example that ties the whole BI/analytics pipeline together: Purina (a pet/animal nutrition company) built the PANDA platform to predict an animal's final weight after a 63-day feeding trial from its eating behavior — informing new-product (feed) development.

Figure 1 — PANDA Platform Architecture (four layers)

The architecture mirrors the generic BI stack: capture → store/clean → analyze → visualize.

Data Acquisition Layer
Purina Research Facility. Barns (animals) generate data → data-acquisition software → shared SSD drive → Cloud Data Gateway pushes it to the cloud.
↓
Transactional Layer
Microsoft Azure Cloud. PANDA Data Governance + a Data Quality Manager route data through an Azure SQL database, splitting errored data (sent back for fixes) from clean data. Azure Repos / Pipelines / Function Apps / DevOps + Blob Storage support the flow.
↓
Analytical Layer
Clean data feeds the Analytics engine and analytical layers, landing in an Azure SQL Data Warehouse ready for modeling and querying.
↓
Visualization Layer
Purina Researchers consume results through Power BI — reporting, dashboarding, monitoring, and statistical analysis.
Notice the mapping: this is exactly the BI architecture from §4 in practice — data warehouse (Analytical Layer) → business analytics (Analytics engine) → performance/monitoring → user interface (Power BI dashboards).

This is a textbook predictive-analytics ensemble: several diverse models are blended (averaged) to produce a more robust forecast than any single model.

What can we learn from this story?

  • Data platforms come first. Most organizations still face data issues; building a platform to share all internal and most external data is expensive but crucial to begin any data-science journey.
  • Communication is critical. Constant dialogue between business domain experts and data-science teams drives success.
  • EDA & feature engineering are the keys. Exploratory data analysis and feature engineering matter most for model development.
  • Speed is a payoff. An analytics platform significantly accelerates product experimentation in new-product development.

12. Key Takeaways

  • 1 Decision support has evolved continuously: DSS → EIS → BI → Analytics → Big Data / Data Science.
  • 2 DSS couples human intellect with computers to tackle unstructured problems via Simon's Intelligence → Design → Choice → Implementation cycle.
  • 3 BI is an umbrella term; its goal is easy access to data to turn data → information → decisions → action, resting on four components (DW, analytics, performance/strategy, UI).
  • 4 Analytics comes in three flavors — Descriptive (what happened), Predictive (what will happen), Prescriptive (what to do).
  • 5 Big Data (Volume, Variety, Velocity) is valuable only when analyzed.
  • 6 The analytics ecosystem spans many provider clusters and offers diverse career paths.
