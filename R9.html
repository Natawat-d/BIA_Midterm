One-sentence takeaway: the paper builds a socioeconomic-intelligence database and an integrated DSS that systematically evaluates & selects data-mining tools, combining association-rule mining (Apriori) and classification — and shows its incremental C4.5 model outperforms Logistic Regression by 2.76% on economic intelligence data.

Problem & goal

Information is critical in the economic world; firms must find valuable knowledge for operations quickly & accurately. Current financial surveillance systems often fail to extract pertinent socioeconomic intelligence at high accuracy, and many contemporary methods (e.g., plain logistic regression) handle complex financial datasets poorly. There are also few integrated frameworks that blend association rules, predictive modeling and incremental learning for high-dimensional economic data. Goal: create a socioeconomic-intelligence database, operationalise it as a system, and use a DSS to evaluate & select the optimal data-mining tools for better economic decisions.

Key concepts & definitions

Economic intelligence
Using computer-based data-mining methods/processes for economic research — fusing econometric & data-mining approaches over historical data.
DSS (data-driven)
Interactive systems that assist decision-making; data-driven DSS prioritise access to large structured/time-series data, with OLAP giving the highest functionality.
Association rules
Rules X → Y with support (frequency of the itemset) and confidence (P(Y|X)); found by Apriori via level-wise frequent-itemset search (anti-monotone property).
Incremental learning
Updating a model as new data arrives (here, an incremental C4.5 decision tree) — vital in dynamic economic environments.

The tool-evaluation criteria (the DSS's core)

Because there are many DM algorithms/tools, choosing the right one is hard. The proposed selection methodology evaluates tools against a set of factors:

  • Data mining activities / tasks supported
  • Data models & methodologies
  • Visualization capability
  • Interface approaches
  • Data transfer & import methods / systems
  • Authorization regulations (security/access)

This turns Ch.9's "no single best tool — use a toolbox" into a systematic, multi-criteria selection process.

Method & algorithms

The DSS follows the 5-step DM process: Business Objective → Data Preparation → Mining Method → Evaluation → Integration of Information. The economic-intelligence system for buildings used six main classifications.

  • Algorithms compared: incremental Decision Tree C4.5, Logistic Regression, Naïve Bayes — all in WEKA.
  • Association rules: an optimised Apriori(T) for frequent itemsets (level-wise search, minimum support; e.g., a worked market-basket example with OJ/soda/milk and the co-occurrence table).
  • Validation: k-fold cross-validation (k = 10); metrics = Accuracy and F1-score (= 2·Precision·Recall / (Precision+Recall)).
  • Significance: a paired t-test over multiple runs confirms the 2.76% accuracy improvement is statistically significant (p < 0.05).

Results

The proposed incremental C4.5 algorithm achieved a 2.76% higher classification accuracy than Logistic Regression in the incremental-learning scenario, with comparisons also made to Naïve Bayes and non-incremental variants. The DSS plus OLAP analysis (slice/dice/roll-up/drill-down on the economic cube) supports faster, more accurate economic-intelligence retrieval — and OLAP analysis also helps assess Balanced Scorecard objectives.

Why C4.5 outperformed Logistic Regression (5 reasons)

  1. Non-linear relationships — C4.5 models them; LR assumes a linear decision boundary.
  2. Automatic feature selection — C4.5 splits on information gain; LR needs manual preprocessing/regularisation.
  3. Robustness to outliers/noise — common in economic data; LR is skewed by extremes.
  4. Categorical & mixed data — C4.5 handles natively; LR needs one-hot encoding (↑ dimensionality).
  5. Interpretability — C4.5 yields human-readable decision rules for decision-makers.

Conclusions

The study contributes a hybrid data-mining architecture (association rules + classification + incremental learning) inside a cost-effective, scalable DSS for economic intelligence, plus a fresh multi-criteria methodology to evaluate & select DM tools. It demonstrates superior accuracy (+2.76% over LR) and sets benchmarks for real-time, adaptive analysis of high-velocity financial data — a basis for better financial decision-making.
