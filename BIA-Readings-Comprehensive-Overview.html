R1 A Taxonomy of Decision Support Systems

Steven Alter · Sloan Management Review, Fall 1977

Background / question

"DSS" was not a homogeneous category — systems labelled DSS had little in common ("like discussing pets in general"). The field needed shared vocabulary & a framework.

Method

Analyzed 56 decision support systems (distilled from 8 in-depth cases); tested and rejected common labels; built a type-based taxonomy.

Organizing principle

Classify by the degree of action implication of outputs — from retrieving a data item to making a decision.

Key finding

Seven DSS types on a data-oriented → model-oriented continuum; type predicts users, risks & design choices.

The seven types

FamilyTypeWhat it does (example)
Data-orientedFile DrawerOnline access to individual data items (stock enquiry)
Data AnalysisManipulate current/historical data (budget variance analysis)
Analysis InformationDatabases + small models + external data (marketing IS)
Model-orientedAccounting ModelsConsequences via definitional relationships (P&L estimate)
RepresentationalConsequences via causal/simulation relationships
OptimizationOptimal solution under constraints (LP scheduling)
SuggestionComputes a specific suggested decision (insurance renewal rate)

Why the taxonomy is useful (Alter's 3 uses)

  • Design — sketch a candidate of each type to surface alternative approaches before committing.
  • Implementation — anticipate type-specific risks (e.g. data-oriented = efficient retrieval; model-oriented = model credibility & users grasping the model).
  • Communication & research — a shared language so "DSS" stops meaning wildly different things.
Comparative findings: type predicts the users (data/suggestion systems often run hands-on by line staff; analysis/model systems use a staff analyst as intermediary), the key technical problem (retrieval vs building the model), and the implementation challenge.
Connects to Ch.1: the foundational DSS definition & the structured/unstructured decision distinction; DSS as an umbrella concept.

R2 Business Intelligence & its Pivotal Role in Organizational Performance

Moussas, Hafiane & Achaba · Journal of Autonomous Intelligence, 2024 — exhaustive literature review

Background / question

In a fast-changing, competitive environment, how does BI enhance organizational performance, and what is its multi-dimensional nature?

Method

Systematic literature review of peer-reviewed articles, case studies & seminal works; samples range from small focus groups to large organizational surveys.

Focus themes

BI components; role in strategic decision-making; operational efficiency; metrics & KPIs influenced by BI, across sectors.

Key finding

BI significantly improves strategic decisions & efficiency and impacts KPIs, delivering competitive advantage; "indispensable" for modern organizations.

What the review synthesizes

  • BI is multi-dimensional: architectures, tools, processes — not a single technology.
  • Its value spans strategic (better decisions) and operational (efficiency) levels.
  • Impact shows up in measurable KPIs across many sectors → ties directly to Ch.4's performance-management layer.
Limitation: as a literature review it synthesizes others' findings rather than testing new data; effect sizes vary widely by study and sector.
Connects to Ch.1 & Ch.4: BI as an umbrella concept (Ch.1) and the BPM / Balanced-Scorecard / KPI layer (Ch.4).

R3 Visualization of Data Inventory Using VDM & EDA

Yanto & Handayani · Int. J. Informatics Visualization (JOIV), 2025

Background / question

A skincare clinic could not interpret its inventory data → stock accumulation, unguaranteed availability, lost sales turnover. (1) How to visualize it? (2) What insights result?

Method

Visual Data Mining (VDM) + Exploratory Data Analysis (EDA) in Tableau.

Data / domain

Inventory records, ~95–98 products, Jan 2019 – Dec 2020 (retail skincare clinic).

Key finding

3 dashboards built; most products stable, a handful inefficient; supports data-driven stock decisions.

Method — three phases

  1. Project planning — justification, project plan, identify the top business question.
  2. Data preparation — choose, transform & verify the dataset.
  3. Data analysis — choose visualization tool, analyze & evaluate the visuals, verify against business objectives.
Results & metrics: three dashboards (product pattern · sales turnover · sales efficiency). Of 95 products, 87 stable, 8 high-fluctuation. Efficient: Ng-Neher Pagi, Ng Badan Pagi, Naavagreen Moist Aha Cream (e.g. 1,332 stock vs 637 sales → small gap). Inefficient: Ng Facial Wash, Ng Skin Toner, Ng Moisturizing Sunscreen (stock-sales imbalance). Highest turnover rise +12.86% (Jan 2020). Validated by the clinic's admin team.
TableauVDMEDADashboardsDescriptive analytics
Connects to Ch.3: data visualization & "insight, not pictures"; descriptive analytics turning raw data into actionable visuals.

R4 Visual Analytic for Traffic Impact Assessment

Chan, Fahad, Goh & Tee · Int. J. Informatics Visualization (JOIV), 2024 · Multimedia University, Malaysia

Background / question

Rising vehicle ownership → congestion & incidents. Traffic Impact Assessment (TIA) outputs are raw numbers users can't easily read. Can visual analytics make TIA actionable?

Method

Spatial + temporal visual analytics after data cleaning, integration & transformation.

Data / domain

Traffic flow at three major intersections (lane counts, vehicle types, saturation flow, incidents).

Key finding

Distinct peak/off-peak & weekday/weekend patterns; congested junctions identified via Level of Service.

Charts & analysis used

  • Heat maps — traffic volume by hour/day to expose peaks.
  • Parallel-coordinate charts — multidimensional comparison across lanes/vehicle types.
  • Bar plots — junction-by-junction volume comparison.
  • Level of Service (LOS) derived from the volume-to-capacity (V/C) ratio to grade each junction.
Results: remarkable volume/pattern differences between peak and off-peak (weekday vs weekend); the V/C ratio flagged potential congested areas; incident analysis showed effects on flow → a scaffold for predictive, proactive traffic control.
Limitation / future: current work is descriptive visual analytics; the authors call for predictive models built on these findings for real-time decision-making & sustainable traffic strategies.
Heat mapsParallel coordinatesBar plotsV/C ratio → LOSDSS
Connects to Ch.3: visual analytics = information visualization moving toward predictive analytics; supports the decision-making layer.

R5 Data Warehouse and Data Mining for Business Intelligence

Kumar, Gupta & Saste · VIVA-Tech Int. Journal for Research & Innovation, 2024

Background / question

How do data warehouse + data mining together strengthen business-intelligence capability for decision-making & competitive advantage?

Method

Review / synthesis: DW architecture (incl. star/snowflake) + DM techniques as supervised / unsupervised / semi-supervised.

Data / domain

Case studies across retail, healthcare, financial services.

Key finding

Together they enable customer-behavior analysis, disease diagnosis & fraud detection — but face real implementation challenges.

DW architecture components

Data sources → ETL → centralized warehouse (dimensional model: star/snowflake) → analysis/reporting; optimized for querying, not transactions.

Challenges & limitations (7)

  • Data quality & consistency (missing/inaccurate values) · Scalability & performance (slow queries as volume grows)
  • Privacy & security (DWs are breach targets) · Integration complexity (heterogeneous/legacy sources)
  • Skilled-personnel shortage · Cost / resource constraints · Governance & compliance (GDPR, HIPAA)
Future directions: real-time data streams (IoT/sensors/social), big-data integration, and machine-learning automation of the analytics pipeline.

R6 Using Data Mining to Predict Student Performance

Zahruddin et al. · Int. J. Informatics Visualization (JOIV), 2023 — case study, UPNM Malaysia

Background / question

Can student performance be predicted from socio-demographic attributes, and which attributes matter most — so lecturers can intervene for the next batch?

Method

Classification via the KDD process in WEKA: Decision Tree, Neural Network, k-Nearest Neighbours.

Data / domain

UPNM student demographics (gender, age, marital & student status [civilian/cadet], rural/urban) + grades; education data mining.

Key finding

Most influential attributes: gender, age, student status.

Model comparison (by RMSE — lower = better)

  • Neural Network — lowest RMSE = best; captured most demographic variables influencing performance.
  • k-Nearest Neighbour — middle; used 44 k-values; grouping method gave decent accuracy (correlation < 1).
  • Decision Tree — highest RMSE = worst; limited ability, classified only a few attributes at leaf nodes.
Note: the paper frames DT & NN as supervised and kNN as its "unsupervised" comparator; RMSE & correlation coefficient are the accuracy measures.

Connects to Ch.5: supervised classification, model comparison, and error-based accuracy measurement (RMSE).

R8 Characterizing Patients at Higher Cardiovascular Risk for Prescribed Stimulants

Yan, Chen, Nasir, Griffin, Bone & Tuan · Computers in Biology and Medicine, 2025

Background / question

Stimulant prescriptions rose sharply (dosage +250% over a decade). Quantify the added cardiovascular risk and identify who is adversely affected.

Method

Compared several ML models (incl. tree-based gradient boosting & random forest) to predict 1-year CV risk + association rule mining (ARM).

Data / domain

EHR (TriNetX), 219,965 adults with ADHD — 102,138 (46.4%) on stimulants — 2010–2020, split into 4 age groups.

Key finding

Strong prediction + an actionable comorbidity "watch list" per age group.

Why ML over classical models

Prior studies used linear models (Poisson, logistic, Cox) and reached inconsistent conclusions; ML (tree-based) captures non-linear interactions and individualized effects rather than a population average.

Results & metrics: all models reached AUC 0.77–0.84 across age groups. For the top-25%-added-risk patients, ARM surfaced critical features: known CV risk factors, prior CV events, substance-use disorders, psychological disorders. A validated comorbidity watch list covers up to ~20% of the population.
Limitation / future: identified features need external validation before guiding safer prescribing.
Gradient boostingRandom forestAssociation rule miningAUC/ROCEHR
Connects to Ch.5: combines prediction (supervised ML, AUC) with association rules — both core Ch.5 methods — on real structured health data.

R9 Integrated DSS for Evaluating Data-Mining Tools in an Economic Intelligence System

Salah, Ahmed & Layeb · Int. J. Informatics Visualization (JOIV), 2025

Background / question

Economic decisions need fast, accurate retrieval of valuable knowledge. Build a scalable DSS for economic/financial intelligence.

Method

An integrated DSS using DM — association rules + forecasting; benchmarks Naïve Bayes, Logistic Regression, incremental C4.5 decision tree.

Data / domain

Economic/financial intelligence dataset (e.g. retail purchase patterns — orange juice & soda affinities).

Key finding

The incremental C4.5 classifier wins on accuracy (rules are also interpretable).

Results & metrics

  • C4.5 classification accuracy is +2.76% over Logistic Regression (headline), reported up to +3.84% vs LR and +7.87% vs Naïve Bayes in the body.
  • Trade-off: incremental C4.5 needs longer training time, but supports incremental learning.
  • Outputs are association rules → high interpretability (decision rules) for analysts.
Limitation / future: authors recommend cross-validation & formal significance testing to strengthen the accuracy claims.

Background / question

Marketers need topic ideas for text-based Instagram posts, but no SMA tool reads the text inside content images.

Method

A Social Media Analytics tool in Python using OCR (extract text from images) + Topic Modelling via LDA.

Data / domain

Instagram content (image text, captions, likes, comments, upload time, category) collected via Instascrapper.

R11 Social Intelligence and Web Mining for Renewable Energy Insights

Mwafaq, Mool, Mahmoud, Jawad & Sharad · Int. J. Informatics Visualization (JOIV), 2025

Background / question

Public acceptance drives renewable-energy adoption. Mine unstructured online discourse to gauge opinion & predict regional adoption.

Method

A pipeline of web mining + NLP + sentiment classification + topic modeling + regression-based forecasting.

Data / domain

12 months of social media, forums & web news on renewable energy (multi-region).

Key finding

Public discourse structure is strongly linked to regional readiness & adoption.

Analytic framework

  • Web mining gathers unstructured text from multiple sources.
  • Sentiment analysis estimates polarity; topic modeling finds dominant themes.
  • Multivariate regression relates digital sentiment + thematic emphasis to predicted adoption rates, fitted on real regional data.
Results: sentiment is largely positive, especially among European & North American stakeholders; dominant themes = economic benefit & technological advances; positive/benefit-focused discourse correlates with high projected adoption → digital cues predict energy demand.
Limitations / future: linguistic diversity (English-dominated) & model transferability; future = multilingual models, multimodal (visual/acoustic) signals, dynamic sentiment over time around policy events.

R12 Sentiment Analysis & CEEMDAN to Learn Consumer-Group Preferences

Wang, Gong, Wei, Martínez & Herrera-Viedma · Applied Soft Computing, 2026 — online hotel reviews

Background / question

Learn & interpret consumer-group preferences from online reviews that are unstructured, time-varying & ambiguous.

Method

A 3-stage data-driven model: BERT → CEEMDAN → UTA.

Data / domain

Real-world hotel-industry online reviews; consumer-preference / MCDM modeling.

Key finding

Interpretable group preferences + the group's classification value system.

The three stages

  1. BERT (deep learning) — fine-grained, criteria-level sentiment quantified from reviews.
  2. CEEMDAN — decompose the sentiment series; the residual-trend mean ≈ group-consistent sentiment (handles the time-dependence other methods ignore).
  3. UTA (UTilités Additives) — preference-disaggregation model → infers the group's value system & core priorities.
Why it's novel: fixes three gaps — (1) discrete sentiment classes lose emotional continuity; (2) classic MCDM relies on explicit/fixed preferences; (3) prior work ignores time-dependence. Validated on hotel reviews with strong interpretability.
BERTFine-grained sentimentCEEMDANUTADeep learningMCDM
Connects to Ch.6: the most advanced text-analytics paper — deep-learning (BERT) sentiment analysis feeding a preference-decision model.
