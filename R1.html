One-sentence takeaway: "DSS" is not a homogeneous category; the systems labelled DSS differ greatly in what they do and how, so Alter classifies them along a single spectrum from data-oriented (retrieve/analyse facts) to model-oriented (recommend or make decisions) — and that type predicts the users, the key intermediary role, the technical challenge and the implementation risk.

Problem & motivation

Despite a "growing pains" surge of interest in decision support systems (the term was a 1970s buzzword), there was little organised knowledge about DSS. Systems carrying the DSS label had so little in common that, as Alter puts it, talking about "the decision support system in general" was like "talking about pets in general… distinguishing between dogs and cats and piranha fish and turtles." A great deal of conjecture had been generated about the nature of DSS, often contradictory.

The article reports a taxonomic scheme built from an exploratory field study to (1) gain a better understanding of the dynamics of these systems and (2) identify the key issues behind their success or failure — issues that differ across types of DSS.

DSS vs EDP — the founding distinction

A definitional question arose immediately: what are decision support systems? Business computer applications are stereotyped into two categories:

EDP (Electronic Data Processing)DSS (Decision Support Systems)
Designed toAutomate / expedite transaction processing, record keeping, business reportingAid in decision making & decision implementation
OrientationMechanical efficiency of clerical activitiesOverall effectiveness of individuals / organisations
Usage patternUser receives reports periodicallyUser initiates each instance of use — directly or via an intermediary

Alter notes the DSS/EDP dichotomy is weakened by overlaps, because many systems serve multiple purposes and orientations.

The organizing principle — "degree of action implication"

Alter first tested and rejected several obvious labelling schemes because they failed to group similar systems meaningfully:

  • Functional area (marketing, production, finance)
  • Decision perspective (operational control, management control, strategic planning)
  • Problem type (structured vs unstructured)
  • Computer technology (interactive vs batch)
  • Modeling approach (simulation vs optimization)

Few significant conclusions emerged from these. The scheme that proved most useful classified systems by the degree of action implication of system outputs — i.e., how directly the system's outputs determine the decision. This forms a single spectrum of generic operations, from extremely data-oriented to extremely model-oriented:

  1. Retrieving a single item of information
  2. Providing a mechanism for ad-hoc data analysis
  3. Providing prespecified aggregations of data in the form of reports
  4. Estimating the consequences of proposed decisions
  5. Proposing decisions
  6. Making decisions

The seven types of DSS

The 56 systems fell into seven types, grouped into two families. Figure 1 collapses the taxonomy into a data-oriented vs model-oriented dichotomy (with sub-bands: Data Retrieval, Data Analysis, Simulation, Suggestion).

Family#TypeWhat it does
Data-oriented 1File Drawer systemsMechanised versions of manual filing — immediate on-line access to particular data items (e.g., inventory status, stock portfolios).
2Data Analysis systemsManipulation of current/historical data by operators tailored to the task or of a general nature (e.g., variance reports, financial analysis).
3Analysis Information systemsAccess to a series of databases + small models + relevant external data, to support a particular decision (e.g., marketing information systems).
Model-oriented 4Accounting ModelsCalculate the consequences of planned actions using definitional (accounting) relationships — "what-if" on income statements, budgets, balance sheets.
5Representational ModelsEstimate consequences of actions using models that are partially non-definitional (causal / simulation relationships).
6Optimization ModelsProvide guidelines for action by generating optimal solutions consistent with a series of constraints (e.g., linear programming).
7Suggestion ModelsPerform mechanical work leading to a specific suggested decision for a fairly structured task.
Key contrast: data-oriented systems retrieve and analyse data; model-oriented systems compute consequences, propose, or decide. The seven types form a ladder of increasing "action implication."

The example systems (the 8 in-depth cases & others)

Alter illustrates each type with real, anonymised systems — useful concrete anchors:

A · File Drawer

  • A1 — CRT-based inventory control in complex manufacturing; a missing part halts assembly, so knowing location/status of all parts is vital (13 standard retrieval commands).
  • A2 — Shop-floor information system tracking integrated-circuit production through ~50 steps to detect yield problems.

B · Data Analysis

  • B1 — Bank system letting division comptrollers generate & annotate customised monthly variance reports (vs budget).
  • B2 — Generalised financial-analysis "interpretive language" for analysts to evaluate investment opportunities / consolidate plans.

C · Analysis Information

  • C1 — Growing marketing information system in consumer products (internal sales + purchased competitive data via report generator + stats package) for forecasting & competitive analysis.
  • C2 — Sales-analysis system at an industrial-equipment company adding purchased external info + industry-sector growth forecasts to support planning.

D · Accounting Models

  • D1 — Voyage-profitability estimator (shipping) computing standard profit for a voyage to decide what charter rate to charge.
  • D2 — On-line source & application of funds budget over a one-year horizon (insurance), minimising idle cash.

E · Representational Models

  • E1 — Chemical company simulates cost flows through a hierarchy of mining sites / production facilities to evaluate yearly budgeting alternatives.
  • E2 — Aggregate market-response model relating advertising/promotion/pricing to sales for a consumer brand.

F · Optimization Models

  • F1 — Determines start dates of training classes under high attrition to meet forecasted staffing needs (smoothing algorithm).
  • F2 — Linear-programming model generating optimal product recipes at minimum cost as raw-material availability/supply fluctuates.

G · Suggestion Models

  • G1 — Computes group-insurance renewal rates from historical premium/claim relationships, replacing manual underwriting (underwriter reviews & can override).
  • G2 — Expedites assembly of electronic equipment: with 200 diode resonant frequencies and millions of combinations, LP maximises units built and auto-generates circuit diagrams.

Comparative findings (Figure 2)

By asking what type of operation a system performs, Alter sorts the 56 systems and characterises each type along several dimensions. Patterns:

DimensionPattern across the types
Type of taskOperational (File Drawer, Suggestion) → planning/analysis (most model types).
Hands-on userNonmanagerial line/staff personnel for data & suggestion systems; staff analysts / managers for analysis & model systems.
Key roleDirect hands-on use for A & G; intermediaries (structured, performed by staff) are central to types C–F → effective communication with the decision maker matters most there.
Key usage problemOperational systems (A, G): user motivation & training (development often initiated by superiors). Data-analysis (B): a recurrent problem is assuming users will "figure out" how to apply the system.
Key design/implementation issueFile-drawer (A): defining the data & handling day-to-day procedural changes. Representational/optimization (E, F): lack of understanding of how the model works → must be well represented to intermediaries.
Key technical problemData-oriented: attaining flexibility & efficiency in retrieval from a data base. Model-oriented: developing the model itself (modeling methods were still insufficient for many analyses).

Crucially, taxonomic contingencies (not noise) explain much of the variation in success — i.e., the type drives which issues dominate.

Conclusions & the three uses of the taxonomy

Alter argues a taxonomy is appropriate & useful, having proceeded by: choosing an organising principle (generic operations) → describing the taxonomy → giving examples of each type → comparing types by characteristics. He claims the taxonomy is useful in three ways:

  1. A guideline for designing systems — a designer can sketch a system of each type as a potential solution and then combine the most useful features (generating alternative designs) before settling on a final design.
  2. A guideline for implementing systems — because implementation issues vary by type, the taxonomy gives advance warning of the difficulties symptomatic of each type.
  3. A framework for communication & research — it reduces confusion around overloaded terms (MIS, "interactive processing", DSS) by making the connotations type-specific; e.g., a File-Drawer user and a large optimization-model user have very different views of what "DSS" means.
Why it still matters: Alter's "degree of action implication" spectrum is an early ancestor of today's descriptive → predictive → prescriptive analytics ladder — support that ranges from retrieving facts to recommending the decision.

Key terms

DSS
Interactive computer-based system that aids decision making/implementation; user initiates each use.
EDP
Electronic Data Processing — automates transactions/record-keeping for efficiency.
Action implication
How directly a system's output determines the decision — the taxonomy's axis.
Intermediary
A staff person who operates the system on the decision maker's behalf (central to types C–F).
Definitional vs non-definitional model
Accounting models use definitional (identity) relationships; representational models use causal/simulation relationships.
