Ch.1 Overview of BI, Analytics & Data Science

Key idea: Business Intelligence is the modern evolution of decision support. Its entire purpose is to convert data → information → knowledge → decision → action, and every technique in the course slots into one of three analytics types: descriptive, predictive, prescriptive.

Evolution of decision support

Computerised decision support evolved decade by decade — each era adds a capability rather than replacing the last.

EraWhat appeared & why it matters
1970sMIS / static periodic reports; early DSS for one-off analytical questions.
1980sExecutive Information Systems (EIS) — summarised information for senior managers.
1990sOLAP: dynamic, multidimensional, ad-hoc reporting → the term "BI" was coined by Gartner.
2010s+AI, data/text/web mining, dashboards, Big Data, social-media analytics.

Decision support system (DSS) — definition & components

DSS
Interactive computer-based systems that help decision makers use data and models to solve semi-/unstructured problems (Gorry & Scott-Morton, 1971). Usage: couples human judgment with computing to improve decision quality.
4 DSS components
Data management (internal/external data) · Model management · Knowledge-based subsystem · User interface.

Simon's four-phase decision process

Intelligence
find problem→Design
build model, options→Choice
select option→Implementation
act + monitor

Usage: the canonical way to structure any managerial decision; expect a "name & order the phases" question.

Gorry & Scott-Morton framework — structure × control

Decisions are classified on two axes: degree of structure (structured / semistructured / unstructured) and type of control (operational / managerial / strategic).

Structure ↓ / Control →OperationalManagerialStrategic
StructuredOrder entry, A/RBudget analysis, short-term forecastWarehouse location
SemistructuredProduction scheduling, inventoryCredit evaluationM&A, new products
UnstructuredApproving loansHiring an executiveR&D planning

Business Intelligence framework

BI (broad definition): an umbrella term combining architectures, tools, databases, analytical tools, applications and methodologies — a "content-free expression." Objective: easy access to data so managers can analyse and act.

4 components of a BI system

  • Data warehouse — organised source data (Ch.4).
  • Business analytics — tools to manipulate, mine, analyse.
  • Business Performance Management (BPM) — monitor & analyse performance.
  • User interface — e.g. dashboard.
OLTPOLAP
GoalData captureDecision support
SystemsERP, SCM, CRM, POSData warehouses

BI analytical applications: customer segmentation · propensity to buy · customer profitability · fraud detection · attrition (churn) · channel optimization.

The three analytics types (most-tested figure)

DescriptivePredictivePrescriptive
QuestionWhat happened?What will happen? Why?What should I do? Why?
EnablersReporting, dashboards, scorecards, DWData/text/web mining, forecastingOptimization, simulation, expert systems

Prescriptive analytics uses descriptive + predictive results to create alternatives and pick the best. INFORMS defines analytics as combining computer technology, management science and statistics.

Simon's four phases — in depth

Each phase has a distinct job, and the process is iterative — you can loop back from any later phase to an earlier one when new information appears.

PhaseWhat actually happensDSS support
IntelligenceScan reality; identify & classify the problem (or opportunity); decompose it; establish ownership. Output = a problem statement.Data/text/web mining, reporting, dashboards, OLAP — surface the symptom.
DesignBuild a model of the problem; set criteria; generate alternatives; predict each outcome. Output = a set of alternatives.Model management, what-if/optimization, simulation, forecasting.
ChoiceSearch/evaluate/recommend a course of action against the criteria; do sensitivity analysis. Output = a chosen alternative.Optimization solvers, scoring, sensitivity analysis.
ImplementationPut the decision to work; monitor results; if it fails, return to an earlier phase. Output = a solution in action.Dashboards/KPIs, BPM, alerts — close the loop.

A fifth implicit step — Monitoring — is really intelligence applied to the implemented solution. Decisions are structured (routine, all phases programmable), unstructured (none are), or semistructured (some are) — DSS targets the semistructured/unstructured middle.

DSS — characteristics & capabilities

  • Supports semistructured/unstructured problems by combining human judgment with computer data/models.
  • Supports all managerial levels (operational → strategic) and all decision phases (intelligence → implementation).
  • Supports individuals and groups; interdependent and/or sequential decisions.
  • Adaptive, flexible, user-controlled (the manager — not the system — is in charge); easy to build/modify; improves effectiveness (better decisions) over efficiency (lower cost).
DSS ≠ MIS ≠ EDP. EDP/TPS automate routine transactions (efficiency); MIS produces standard periodic reports for structured problems; DSS supports non-routine, semistructured decisions with models + interaction. BI is the modern descendant that fuses all three with a data warehouse.

BI vs Analytics vs Data Science — the vocabulary trap

The exam likes "BI is an umbrella/content-free term" and the idea that analytics = the engine inside BI.

They are cumulative: prescriptive consumes descriptive + predictive results to generate and choose alternatives. Maturity rises with both difficulty and value from descriptive → prescriptive.

"Sexiest job of the 21st century." A blend of skills — domain expertise + maths/statistics + programming/databases + ML + communication/storytelling + curiosity/skepticism. Ch.3 Nature of Data, Statistical Modeling & Visualization (Descriptive Analytics I)

Key idea: Descriptive analytics answers "what happened?" — but you must first understand the nature of the data (types & measurement scales) and clean it. Statistics summarise the data; visualization communicates it for insight.

Nature & taxonomy of data

Data is the lowest level of abstraction from which information and knowledge derive. Quality & integrity are critical — "garbage in, garbage out."

Structured (for computers)Unstructured / semi-structured (for humans)
Categorical: nominal, ordinal · Numerical: interval, ratioText, multimedia (image/audio/video), XML/JSON, HTML, log files

Scales of measurement (cumulative properties)

ScaleNames?Order?Equal gaps?True zero / ratios?
Nominal✓———
Ordinal✓✓——
Interval✓✓✓—
Ratio✓✓✓✓
Example: jersey numbers = nominal (labels). Survey rank 1–5 = ordinal (order, but gaps not equal). Temperature in °C = interval (0° isn't "no temperature", so 20° ≠ twice 10°). Weight/kg = ratio (true zero → 20 kg is twice 10 kg). Usage: the scale dictates which statistics and chart types are valid.

Data preprocessing — the "art & science"

Real-world data is dirty, misaligned, complex, inaccurate. Four tasks (with feedback loops) make it analytics-ready:

TaskMethods / usage
ConsolidationAccess, select, integrate (SQL, web services, ontology mapping).
CleaningImpute missing values (mean/median/mode), reduce noise/outliers, remove erroneous records.
TransformationNormalize (e.g. 0–1), discretize/aggregate (binning), construct new attributes.
ReductionReduce attributes (PCA, correlation), reduce records (sampling), balance skewed data (over/under-sample).

Statistical modeling & regression

Statistics = techniques to characterise & interpret data. Two branches: descriptive (describe the data as-is) and inferential (conclude about a population from a sample).

AspectMeasures
Central tendencyMean, median, mode
DispersionRange, variance, standard deviation, quartiles, box-and-whisker plot
ShapeHistogram; skewness (asymmetry); kurtosis (peakedness)

Regression — the most widely used technique

  • Simple linear y = β₀ + β₁x; multiple linear y = β₀ + β₁x₁ + … + βₙxₙ.
  • Fit by ordinary least squares (OLS) — the line minimising the sum of squared errors.
  • Used for explanation (hypothesis testing) and forecasting (prediction).
  • Quality: R² (variance explained), p-values (significance), errors MSE / MAD / RMSE.

Five assumptions: linearity · independence · normality · constant variance (homoscedasticity) · no multicollinearity.

Logistic regression

A statistics-based classification algorithm (supervised). The target is a binary variable, modelled with the sigmoid f(y)=1/(1+e−(β₀+β₁x)) → outputs a probability 0–1.

Time-series forecasting models a series over time (trend + seasonality) rather than an input→output relationship.

Business reporting & data visualization

Report = Information → Decision. Report types: metric management (KPIs internal / SLAs external), dashboard (many indicators on one page), balanced-scorecard.

"The purpose of visualization is insight, not pictures." — Ben Shneiderman.
  • William Playfair invented the line & pie chart (1801); Minard's map of Napoleon's 1812 march = classic multivariate chart.
  • Visual analytics = information visualization + predictive analytics. Leaders: Tableau, Qlik, Microsoft Power BI, SAS.
  • Dashboard layers: Monitoring · Analysis · Management; good dashboards highlight exceptions, allow drill-down, unify systems, need little training.

"Garbage in, garbage out." Data must be fit for purpose along several dimensions:

Preprocessing — techniques behind each task

IssueHow it's fixed
Missing valuesIgnore/drop the record; fill with mean/median/mode; predict via regression/kNN; use a constant ("unknown").
Noise / outliersBinning (smooth by bin mean/median/boundary), regression smoothing, clustering to isolate outliers, IQR / z-score rules.
NormalizationMin-max to 0–1: x' = (x−min)/(max−min); z-score: x' = (x−μ)/σ; decimal scaling.
Discretization / aggregationBin continuous → categories; roll detail up (day→month).
Dimensionality reductionPCA, attribute subset selection, correlation filtering.
Imbalanced classesOver-sample the minority (SMOTE), under-sample the majority, or re-weight.

Descriptive statistics — the measures, precisely

FamilyMeasureNote
Central tendencyMeanSensitive to outliers.
MedianRobust middle value; use for skewed data.
ModeMost frequent; only one valid for nominal data.
DispersionRange / IQRIQR = Q3 − Q1; basis of the box plot & outlier fences (±1.5·IQR).
Variance / SDSD = √variance; spread in original units.
Quartiles / percentilesShown by box-and-whisker plot.
ShapeSkewnessAsymmetry: right-skew → mean > median.
KurtosisPeakedness / tail heaviness.

Correlation (Pearson r, −1…+1) measures the strength & direction of a linear relationship — correlation ≠ causation. Distinguish descriptive statistics (summarise the sample) from inferential (generalise to a population via confidence intervals & hypothesis tests).

Regression — interpretation & diagnostics

  • Coefficients (β): β₁ = expected change in y per 1-unit change in x (others held constant); β₀ = intercept.
  • R²: proportion of variance in y explained (0–1). Adjusted R² penalises extra predictors.
  • p-value: probability the coefficient is really zero; < 0.05 ⇒ "significant."
  • Error metrics: MAE/MAD (mean absolute error), MSE (squared — punishes big misses), RMSE (same units as y).

Five assumptions (LINEN): Linearity · Independence of errors · Normality of residuals · Equal variance (homoscedasticity) · No multicollinearity. Violations bias the estimates or their significance.

Logistic regression — the odds view

For a binary target, logistic regression models the log-odds (logit): ln(p/(1−p)) = β₀ + β₁x, so p = 1/(1+e−(β₀+β₁x)) (sigmoid, output 0–1). A threshold (e.g. 0.5) turns the probability into a class. It is a statistics-based classifier (supervised), not a numeric predictor — a frequent exam contrast with linear regression.

Time-series forecasting

Models a variable over time using its own history. Components: level, trend, seasonality, cyclical, noise. Methods: moving average, exponential smoothing, ARIMA. Contrast: regression maps inputs→output; time series maps past→future of one series.

Visualization — principles & dashboard design

  • "Insight, not pictures" (Shneiderman). Choose the chart by the analytical question, not by what looks pretty.
  • Avoid chart-junk, 3-D distortion, truncated/dual axes, misleading area scaling.
  • Visual analytics = information visualization + predictive analytics — interactive, exploratory, often real-time.
  • Dashboard best practice: single screen, KPIs vs targets, highlight exceptions, enable drill-down, unify disparate sources, minimal training; three layers — monitoring → analysis → management.
  • Storytelling with data: context → conflict → resolution; one message per view.
Application cases: Student retention (3.2) — predicting freshman attrition; highlights the data-imbalance problem (an 80/20 set can score 90% accuracy yet 50% precision on the minority class; balancing fixes it); compares ANN, decision tree, SVM, logistic regression, ensembles. NCAA bowl games (3.4) — the same outcome modelled as classification (win/loss) vs regression (point-difference); direct classification with a decision tree scored best (~86%).
Exam focus: measurement scales + which is which (interval vs ratio trap); the 4 preprocessing tasks; regression equation, OLS, R²/p-value, the 5 assumptions; logistic regression = binary classification; "insight not pictures"; matching chart to purpose; data-imbalance (accuracy vs precision).

Ch.4 Data Warehousing & Business Performance Management (Descriptive Analytics II)

Key idea: The data warehouse is BI's cleansed, integrated, historical backbone. ETL feeds it, dimensional modelling + OLAP make analysis fast, and a performance-management layer (BPM, KPIs, scorecards, Six Sigma) turns analysis into managed action.

Data warehouse & data marts

Data Warehouse (DW): a collection of integrated, subject-oriented databases supporting DSS, where each unit is non-volatile and tied to a moment in time — a repository of cleansed, standardized relational data.

Characteristics: subject-oriented · integrated · time-variant · nonvolatile · summarized · not normalized · metadata-rich · client/server, web-based, real-time.

ComponentMeaning / usage
Dependent data martSubset created directly from the DW.
Independent data martSmall standalone DW for one unit/department.
ODS (operational data store)Interim staging area for the DW.
EDWEnterprise DW — for the whole organisation.
Metadata"Data about data" — describes DW contents & use.

Architectures & development models

3-tier: data-acquisition (back-end) → the data warehouse → client (front-end) for access & analysis. Five alternatives: independent marts · data-mart bus (conformed dimensions) · hub-and-spoke (corporate information factory) · centralized · federated.

BPM, Balanced Scorecard & Six Sigma

Business Performance Management (BPM) alerts managers to opportunities/problems/threats and empowers response via models & collaboration (also CPM/EPM/SEM).

1 Strategize→2 Plan→3 Monitor/Analyze→4 Act/Adjust

KPI = strategic objective + metric vs a goal. Outcome KPIs = lagging (e.g. revenue); Driver KPIs = leading (e.g. sales leads).

Balanced Scorecard (Kaplan & Norton, 1992) — 4 perspectives

Financial (appear to shareholders) · Customer (appear to customers) · Internal Business Process (excel at) · Learning & Growth (sustain change) — all around Vision & Strategy.

Six Sigma

Reduce defects toward zero DPMO (defects per million opportunities) via the DMAIC loop: Define → Measure → Analyze → Improve → Control. Balanced Scorecard = strategic management system; Six Sigma = performance measurement system.

DW characteristics — each one explained (Inmon's definition)

CharacteristicMeaning
Subject-orientedOrganised around business subjects (customer, product, sales) — not applications.
IntegratedConsistent naming, formats, units across all sources (the hard part of ETL).
Time-variantStores history — every record tied to a moment in time (enables trends).
NonvolatileRead-only for users; loaded then not updated/deleted in place.
Summarized / not normalizedPre-aggregated, denormalized for query speed (vs OLTP's normalized efficiency).
Metadata-rich"Data about data" — describes source, meaning, lineage, usage.

ETL — the integration backbone, step by step

  • Extract — pull from heterogeneous sources (OLTP DBs, files, APIs) into a staging area / ODS.
  • Transform — clean, standardise, deduplicate, derive, conform dimensions, apply business rules (the bulk of the effort).
  • Load — write into the DW; full vs incremental loads; capture only changes (CDC).
  • Modern variant ELT loads raw first then transforms inside a powerful warehouse/lake. Related: EAI (push source data in), EII (real-time virtual integration).

Dimensional modelling — star vs snowflake (worked)

A fact table holds numeric measures (sales amount, qty) + foreign keys; dimension tables hold descriptive context (Product, Store, Date, Customer).

  • Star — dimensions are single, denormalized tables. Fewer joins → fastest queries; some redundancy.
  • Snowflake — dimensions normalized into sub-tables (Product → Category → Department). Less redundancy; more joins → slower.
  • Grain = the level of detail of one fact row (e.g. "one line item per receipt"). Define it first.

OLAP storage types

TypeStorageTrade-off
MOLAPPre-computed multidimensional cubeFastest queries; storage/processing heavy.
ROLAPRelational tables (star schema)Scales to large data; slower.
HOLAPHybrid — summary in cube, detail in relationalBalance of both.
OLAP ops in action: start with Sales by Region×Quarter. Slice = Q1 only; Dice = {Q1,Q2}×{North,South}; Drill-down Region→City; Roll-up City→Region; Pivot = swap rows/columns.

BPM — closing the strategy-to-execution gap

Business Performance Management (a.k.a. CPM/EPM) wraps the analytics in a closed loop: Strategize → Plan → Monitor/Analyze → Act/Adjust. It fixes the common failure where strategy is set but never executed/measured.

KPIs — designed well

  • KPI = strategic objective + a metric measured against a target (a metric without a target/strategy is just a measure).
  • Outcome (lagging) KPIs report results (revenue, churn); Driver (leading) KPIs predict them (sales leads, pipeline) — you need both.
  • Good KPIs are SMART: Specific, Measurable, Achievable, Relevant, Time-bound.

Balanced Scorecard — the cause-and-effect logic

Kaplan & Norton's four perspectives are linked by a strategy map: investment in Learning & Growth (skills, systems) improves Internal Processes → which raises Customer satisfaction → which drives Financial results. So non-financial, leading measures cause the lagging financial ones.

PerspectiveStrategic question
FinancialHow do we look to shareholders?
CustomerHow do customers see us?
Internal Business ProcessWhat must we excel at?
Learning & GrowthCan we keep improving & creating value?
Dashboard vs scorecard: a dashboard is operational/tactical — many real-time indicators, "how are we doing now?"; a scorecard is strategic — periodic objectives vs targets, "are we doing the right things?" BSC is a scorecard methodology.

Six Sigma — DMAIC + the metric

Six Sigma = 3.4 DPMO (≈ 99.99966% good). DPMO = (defects / (units × opportunities-per-unit)) × 1,000,000. The DMAIC cycle improves an existing process; DMADV designs a new one. Often paired with BSC: BSC sets what to improve, Six Sigma how.

Application cases: Maryland targets tax fraud with BI+DW; TELCOs use DW/analytics to stay competitive; BP Lubricants "BIGS" initiative; AARP BI transformation (347% ROI in 3 yrs); Expedia customer-satisfaction scorecard.

Ch.5 Data Mining: Process, Methods & Algorithms (Predictive Analytics I)

Key idea: Data mining is a disciplined, multi-step process (CRISP-DM) — not a crystal ball — for discovering valid, useful patterns in structured data. Its two great families: prediction (classification, regression) and discovery/grouping (clustering, association).

What is data mining?

Definition (Fayyad, 1996): "the nontrivial process of identifying valid, novel, potentially useful, and ultimately understandable patterns in data." A.k.a. knowledge discovery, pattern analysis, information harvesting. It is a blend of disciplines (statistics, ML, AI, databases, visualization). Source is often a data warehouse (not always). DM ≠ statistics: DM discovers patterns in large data, often run by an end user.

Pattern types: association · prediction · cluster (segmentation) · sequential/time-series.

Taxonomy of data-mining tasks

TaskMethodTypical algorithmsLearning
PredictionClassificationDecision trees, ANN, SVM, kNN, Naïve BayesSupervised
RegressionLinear/nonlinear regression, regression trees, ANN, SVMSupervised
Time seriesExponential smoothing, ARIMA, autoregressiveSupervised
AssociationMarket-basketApriori, Eclat, FP-GrowthUnsupervised
Link / sequenceApriori, graph-based matchingUnsupervised
SegmentationClusteringK-means, Expectation-MaximizationUnsupervised
Outlier analysisK-means, EMUnsupervised
Two key contrasts: classification vs regression → categorical vs numeric output. Classification vs clustering → supervised (has labelled target) vs unsupervised (no target).

Standard processes

CRISP-DM (most popular) — six phases; the first three consume ~85% of project time:

1 Business Understanding→2 Data Understanding→3 Data Preparation→4 Model Building→5 Testing & Evaluation→6 Deployment

Highly iterative/experimental. Alternatives: SEMMA (SAS: Sample-Explore-Modify-Model-Assess) and KDD (Knowledge Discovery in Databases).

Classification — methods & evaluation

The most-used DM method: supervised, learns from past labelled data, output is categorical. Assess on accuracy, speed, robustness, scalability, interpretability.

Confusion matrix & metrics

Actual PositiveActual Negative
Predicted PositiveTPFP
Predicted NegativeFNTN
  • Accuracy = (TP+TN)/(TP+TN+FP+FN)
  • Precision = TP/(TP+FP) — of predicted positives, how many correct.
  • Recall / TPR = TP/(TP+FN) — of actual positives, how many caught.
  • True Negative Rate = TN/(TN+FP)
Worked example: TP=80, FP=20, FN=10, TN=90. Accuracy=(80+90)/200=85%; Precision=80/100=80%; Recall=80/90≈89%. Note: with imbalanced classes, high accuracy can hide poor precision — see Ch.3 student-retention case.

Estimation methodologies

  • Simple split / holdout — train ~⅔ (70%), test ⅓ (30%). For neural nets: 60/20/20 (train/validation/test).
  • k-fold cross-validation — k train/test rounds (rotation estimation).
  • Leave-one-out (k = n) · bootstrapping (sample with replacement) · jackknifing.
  • AUC / ROC curve — area under Receiver Operating Characteristics; 0.5 = chance, 1.0 = perfect; robust to skewed classes.

Decision trees

Divide-and-conquer: create a root, pick the best splitting attribute, branch, repeat until each leaf is one class. Algorithms differ on splitting, stopping, and pruning (pre- vs post-pruning to avoid overfitting). Popular: ID3, C4.5, C5, CART, CHAID, M5.

Ensembles combine many models for more robust prediction: bagging (→ random forest), boosting, information fusion.

Clustering & association rules (unsupervised)

Cluster analysis

Finds natural groupings; no target variable; in marketing = segmentation. Methods: k-means / k-modes (statistical), SOM & ART (neural), fuzzy c-means, GA. "How many clusters?" is a key practical question.

k-Means algorithm: (0) choose k → (1) randomly place k centers → (2) assign each point to nearest center → (3) recompute centers → repeat 2–3 until assignments stabilise.

Association rule mining (market-basket)

Finds affinities between items — the famous "diapers & beer." A rule is X → Y [S%, C%]:

  • Support (S) = how often X and Y occur together = P(X∩Y).
  • Confidence (C) = how often Y occurs given X = P(Y|X).
Example: {Laptop, Antivirus} → {Extended Service Plan} [30%, 70%] means the trio appears in 30% of baskets, and 70% of laptop+antivirus buyers also buy the plan. Actions: bundle them, place together — or place apart to force a walk past other goods.

Algorithms: Apriori (bottom-up; build frequent itemsets one item at a time, testing each level against minimum support), Eclat, FP-Growth.

CRISP-DM — what each phase actually does

PhaseKey tasks
1 Business UnderstandingDefine objectives & success criteria; translate the business problem into a data-mining goal.
2 Data UnderstandingCollect, describe, explore (EDA), assess quality — first look at the data.
3 Data PreparationSelect, clean, construct, integrate, format — the "85% of the time" step (with #2).
4 Model BuildingPick technique(s), design the train/test setup, build & tune models.
5 Testing & EvaluationAssess accuracy/quality against the business objective; decide go/no-go.
6 DeploymentPut the model to use; monitor & maintain; report. Loops back as needed.

Alternatives: SEMMA (Sample · Explore · Modify · Model · Assess — SAS) and KDD (selection → preprocessing → transformation → data mining → interpretation/evaluation).

Classification algorithms — how each works

AlgorithmIdeaStrength / watch-out
Decision treeRecursively split on the attribute that best separates classes.Interpretable ("white box"); overfits without pruning.
Naïve BayesApply Bayes' theorem assuming features are independent.Fast, good for text; the independence assumption is "naïve."
k-NNClassify by majority vote of the k nearest neighbours (distance-based).No training ("lazy"); sensitive to k, scaling, irrelevant features.
SVMFind the maximum-margin hyperplane; kernels handle non-linearity.Strong in high dimensions; less interpretable.
ANNLayers of weighted neurons trained by backpropagation.Very flexible; "black box," needs lots of data.

How a decision tree picks splits

  • Information gain / Entropy (ID3, C4.5/C5): pick the split that most reduces entropy −Σ pᵢ log₂ pᵢ.
  • Gini index (CART): pick the split that most reduces impurity 1 − Σ pᵢ².
  • χ² (CHAID). Three design choices: splitting rule, stopping rule, and pruning (pre- vs post-) to fight overfitting.

Ensembles — wisdom of crowds

  • Bagging — train many models on bootstrap samples, average/vote → Random Forest (bagged trees + random feature subsets). Reduces variance.
  • Boosting — train models sequentially, each fixing the prior's errors (AdaBoost, Gradient Boosting, XGBoost). Reduces bias.
  • Stacking / information fusion — a meta-model combines several base models.

Overfitting, underfitting & the bias–variance trade-off

  • Overfitting — model memorises noise; great on training, poor on test (high variance). Fix: more data, pruning/regularisation, cross-validation, simpler model.
  • Underfitting — model too simple to capture the pattern (high bias). Fix: richer model/features.
  • Goal = generalisation: low error on unseen data. This is why we hold out a test set and use k-fold cross-validation.

Clustering — k-means in detail

Distance measures: Euclidean (default), Manhattan, cosine. Choosing k: the elbow method (plot within-cluster SSE vs k, pick the bend) or silhouette score. Limitations: must pre-set k, sensitive to initial seeds & outliers, assumes spherical clusters → density methods (DBSCAN) handle arbitrary shapes (see R7). Always standardise features first so scale doesn't dominate.

Association rules — support, confidence & lift

For a rule X → Y over transactions:

  • Support = P(X∩Y) — how frequent the itemset is (filters rarity).
  • Confidence = P(Y|X) — reliability of the rule (but ignores how common Y is).
  • Lift = Confidence / P(Y) = P(X∩Y)/(P(X)P(Y)). Lift > 1 = positive association, = 1 independent, < 1 negative. Lift fixes confidence's blind spot.

Apriori principle: any subset of a frequent itemset is itself frequent (so supersets of an infrequent set can be pruned). The algorithm grows itemsets level by level, pruning anything below min-support, then generates rules above min-confidence. FP-Growth avoids candidate generation via an FP-tree (faster on big data).

Evaluation — beyond accuracy

  • ROC curve plots TPR vs FPR across thresholds; AUC summarises it (0.5 = random, 1.0 = perfect) and is robust to class imbalance.
  • F1 = 2·(Precision·Recall)/(Precision+Recall) — harmonic mean, the go-to single number for imbalanced classes.
  • Holdout (e.g. 70/30) vs k-fold CV (rotate the test fold k times, average) vs leave-one-out vs bootstrap.
Application cases: Visa — predictive analytics + in-memory analytics to cut fraud while improving service; Dell — analytics for agility; cancer research — ANN vs Logistic Regression vs Random Forest (accuracy/sensitivity/specificity + variable importance); Influence Health — factors influencing healthcare decisions; predicting movie financial success — a 9-class classification problem in IBM SPSS Modeler; opening vignette Miami-Dade Police — predictive policing.

Ch.6 Text, Web & Social Media Analytics (Predictive Analytics II)

Key idea: ~85–90% of corporate data is unstructured text. The whole game is to impose structure on it (the term-document matrix) and then mine that structure for topics, sentiment, and web/social patterns.

Text analytics vs text mining vs data mining

  • Text Analytics = Information Retrieval + Information Extraction + Data Mining + Web Mining (or simply IR + Text Mining).
  • Text mining = a semi-automated process of extracting knowledge from unstructured sources (a.k.a. knowledge discovery in textual databases).
  • Data mining vs text mining — both find novel patterns and are semi-automated; the difference is structured (databases) vs unstructured (Word/PDF/XML) data.

Application areas: information extraction · topic tracking · summarization · categorization · clustering · concept linking · question answering. Domains: law, research, finance, medicine, marketing; email spam filtering & prioritisation.

Web mining & social media analytics

Building structure: the term-document matrix & TF-IDF

Text is unusable until it's numeric. The Term-by-Document Matrix (TDM) = rows (documents) × columns (terms), cells = weights. The standard weight is TF-IDF:

  • TF (term frequency) — how often a term appears in a document (sometimes log-scaled).
  • IDF (inverse document frequency) = log(N / dfₜ) — down-weights terms common across all documents.
  • TF-IDF = TF × IDF — high when a term is frequent in this doc but rare overall ⇒ distinctive. The TDM is huge & sparse, so reduce it with SVD (like PCA for text).

Pre-processing pipeline (turns raw text into terms)

Tokenize → lowercase → remove stop-words → stem/lemmatize → (POS tag, n-grams) → weight (TF-IDF) → reduce (SVD). Stemming chops to a root (running→run); lemmatization maps to a dictionary form (better→good).

Topic modelling — LSA/LSI vs LDA

  • LSA/LSI — apply SVD to the TF-IDF matrix to find latent "concept" dimensions; algebraic, deterministic.
  • LDA (Latent Dirichlet Allocation) — probabilistic generative model: each document = a mixture of topics, each topic = a distribution over words. The most popular topic model (used in R10). You set the number of topics k.

Sentiment analysis — lexicon vs machine learning

ApproachHowTrade-off

Machine-learningTrain a classifier (NB, SVM, or deep learning / BERT) on labelled text.Accurate & context-aware; needs labelled data (see R12).

Four-step process: (1) sentiment detection — objective fact vs subjective opinion (S–O); (2) N–P polarity — negative…positive; (3) target identification — what the opinion is about (aspect-based); (4) aggregation word→sentence→document. Applications: Voice of Customer/Market/Employee, brand & reputation, finance, politics.

Web mining — three branches, with the link algorithms

BranchSourceGoal / technique

  • PageRank (Google) — a page is important if important pages link to it; importance flows along links (recursive, like a random surfer's visit probability).
  • HITS — separates hubs (pages that link to many good pages) from authorities (pages many good hubs point to).

R1 A Taxonomy of Decision Support Systems — Alter (1977)

Problem: "DSS" was not a homogeneous category — systems labelled DSS had little in common ("like discussing pets in general"). The field needed shared vocabulary.

Method: analysed 56 DSS (distilled from 8 in-depth cases); tested & rejected common labels (functional area, decision perspective, problem type, technology); built a type-based taxonomy.

Organizing principle: the degree of action implication of outputs — from retrieving a data item → ad-hoc analysis → reports → estimating consequences → proposing → making decisions.

The seven types (data-oriented → model-oriented):

FamilyType & what it does
Data-orientedFile Drawer — online access to data items (stock enquiry).
Data Analysis — manipulate current/historical data (variance analysis).
Analysis Information — databases + small models + external data (marketing IS).
Model-orientedAccounting Models — consequences via definitional relationships.
Representational Models — consequences via causal/simulation relationships.
Optimization Models — optimal solution under constraints (LP).
Suggestion Models — compute a specific suggested decision (renewal rates).

Findings: type predicts users, the key intermediary role, technical problem, and implementation risk. Usefulness: guides design, implementation, and communication/research. ↔ Ch.1 foundational DSS theory.

Deeper detail

  • Why a taxonomy at all: "DSS" had become a label with no shared meaning. Alter first rejected classification by functional area, decision perspective, or technology because they don't predict design/implementation issues — then chose the output's action implication as the organising axis.
  • Two opposing families: data-oriented systems retrieve/analyse data; model-oriented systems compute consequences or recommendations. The seven types form a spectrum from passive data access → active decision suggestion.
  • Predictive power: a system's type forecasts who uses it, the intermediary role, the technical challenge, and the implementation risk — that's what makes the taxonomy useful, not just descriptive.

  • Limitation: needs some programming proficiency to operate; OCR/scraping fragility — practical, not just algorithmic, constraints.
Exam angle: "describe how topics are identified & the techniques used" — name LDA + OCR and the dual evaluation (expert + SUS); classify as Ch.6 social-media text mining.

R12 Sentiment Analysis & CEEMDAN to Learn Consumer-Group Preferences — Wang et al. (2026)

Problem: learn & interpret consumer-group preferences from unstructured, time-varying, ambiguous online reviews.

Method: BERT (deep-learning) for fine-grained sentiment → CEEMDAN to extract a residual trend (group-consistent sentiment) → UTA method for preference disaggregation.

Data / domain: real-world hotel-industry online reviews; consumer-preference modeling.

Findings: improves the interpretability of group preferences & infers their value system, helping managers identify needs and make evidence-based decisions. ↔ Ch.6 — most advanced text-analytics paper (deep-learning sentiment feeding a decision model).

Deeper detail

  • Three-stage architecture: (1) BERT (BERT-Base-Chinese) does fine-grained, aspect-level sentiment + BERTopic for latent criteria; (2) CEEMDAN decomposes the sentiment time series into intrinsic modes + a residual trend = the group-consistent signal (noise removed); (3) preference disaggregation (UTA-type) infers the group's classification value system (criteria weights).
  • Why BERT over a lexicon: it's contextual (handles negation, sarcasm, aspect) — a deep-learning leap over dictionary methods (links to Ch.6/Ch.7 deep learning).
  • What CEEMDAN adds: reviews are time-varying & noisy; signal decomposition separates the stable preference trend from transient noise — unusual in text analytics, hence "state of the art."
  • Output is a decision model: not just sentiment scores but an interpretable preference/value system for a consumer group → prescriptive-leaning support. Future work: Shapley values, dynamic weights.
Exam angle: "explain the roles of BERT, CEEMDAN & preference disaggregation" — the three-stage pipeline from contextual sentiment → trend extraction → inferred value system.
