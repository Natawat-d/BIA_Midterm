Key idea

Core message: Business Intelligence is the modern evolution of decision support. Its purpose is to convert data → information → knowledge → decision → action, and every technique in the course slots into one of three analytics types: descriptive, predictive, prescriptive.

Chapter 1 sets the vocabulary for the whole course: what decisions are, how computers support them, what BI/analytics/data science/AI mean and how they relate, and the descriptive→predictive→prescriptive ladder.

The changing business environment & the need for analytics

Companies operate in an environment of constant, rapid change (markets, customer demands, technology, regulation, globalisation). Organisations must react quickly, be agile, and make decisions on data, not gut feeling. The pressures–responses–support framework:

  • Pressures — market (competition, globalisation), technology (innovation, obsolescence), societal (regulation, ethics, security).
  • Responses — strategy, partnerships, real-time/on-demand operations, customer focus, mass customisation.
  • Support — computerised decision support / BI / analytics that turn data into action.

Decision making & Simon's process

A decision = choosing among alternatives. Managers' work is decision making; better decisions need data + models. Herbert Simon's four-phase model is the canonical structure:

Intelligence
find/define problem→Design
build model, alternatives→Choice
select best alternative→Implementation
act + monitor
PhaseWhat happens
IntelligenceScan reality; identify & classify the problem/opportunity; ownership.
DesignConstruct a model; set criteria; generate & evaluate alternatives; predict outcomes.
ChoiceSearch/select the recommended solution; sensitivity analysis.
ImplementationPut the decision to work; monitor; loop back if it fails.

The process is iterative — you can return to an earlier phase at any point. Decisions are structured (routine, programmable), unstructured (none of the phases programmable), or semistructured (some are) — BI/DSS targets the semistructured/unstructured middle.

Decision Support System (DSS) — definition & components

DSS (Gorry & Scott-Morton, 1971): interactive, computer-based systems that help decision makers use data and models to solve semistructured & unstructured problems. Aim = improve decision effectiveness (quality), with the human always in control.

Four components

  • Data management — internal & external data (often via a database/DW).
  • Model management — quantitative models & the software to run them.
  • Knowledge-based subsystem — optional expertise/AI component.
  • User interface — dialogue between user and system (e.g. dashboards).

Capabilities

Supports all managerial levels & all decision phases, individuals & groups, interdependent/sequential decisions; adaptive, flexible, user-controlled; improves effectiveness over efficiency.

Evolution of computerised decision support

Each era adds a capability rather than replacing the last:

EraWhat appeared
1970sMIS / static periodic reports; early DSS for one-off analytical questions.
1980sExecutive Information Systems (EIS) — summarised information for senior managers.
1990sOLAP: dynamic, multidimensional, ad-hoc reporting → the term "BI" coined by Gartner (Howard Dresner).
2010s+AI, data/text/web mining, dashboards, Big Data, social-media analytics, cloud, real-time.

Business Intelligence — framework & components

BI (broad definition): an umbrella term combining architectures, tools, databases, analytical tools, applications & methodologies — a "content-free expression." Objective: easy access to data so managers can analyse and act.

Four components of a BI system

  • Data warehouse — organised, integrated source data (Ch.4).
  • Business analytics — tools to manipulate, mine & analyse data.
  • Business Performance Management (BPM) — monitor & analyse performance vs goals.
  • User interface — e.g. dashboards & scorecards.
OLTPOLAP
GoalData capture (transactions)Decision support (analysis)

BI analytical applications: customer segmentation · propensity to buy · customer profitability · fraud detection · attrition (churn) · channel optimisation.

BI vs Analytics vs Data Science: BI = mostly descriptive (reporting/OLAP/dashboards); Analytics = the science spanning descriptive→predictive→prescriptive; Data Science = knowledge extraction from big/unstructured data via ML/AI + programming.

They are cumulative: prescriptive uses descriptive + predictive results to generate and choose alternatives. INFORMS defines analytics as combining computer technology, management science and statistics to solve problems. Maturity & value rise from descriptive → prescriptive.

Big Data & the analytics ecosystem

Big Data = data too large/fast/varied for traditional tools — the Vs:

VolumeScale (TB→PB→ZB)
VarietyStructured + semi/unstructured (text, image, sensor, clickstream)
VelocitySpeed of generation/flow (batch → streaming)
VeracityTrust / quality / uncertainty
ValueWorth extracted — data is worthless until analysed

Enabling tech: Hadoop (HDFS + MapReduce), NoSQL, Hive/Pig, Spark (in-memory), stream processing, the cloud. The analytics ecosystem has 11 player types (infrastructure, DW, middleware, data-service providers; descriptive/predictive/prescriptive software; application developers; user organisations; analysts/influencers; academia; regulators).

AI, machine learning & the data scientist

The 5th-edition framing adds AI as the engine that goes beyond presenting data to recognising patterns, deriving insight and making autonomous decisions. Machine learning & deep learning are AI subsets that learn from data. The data scientist ("sexiest job of the 21st century") blends domain expertise + statistics + programming/databases + ML + communication — at the intersection of computer science, statistics & business.

Application cases

Sabre & Siemens (Dundas BI) — interactive dashboards cut cost; specialty steel — analytics for available-to-promise dates; CenterPoint Energy — real-time Big Data + customer sentiment for outage prediction; athletic injuries — classification + sequence analysis. Pattern: each names a problem, an analytics type, and a measurable payoff.
