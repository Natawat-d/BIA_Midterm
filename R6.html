One-sentence takeaway: using 9 socio-demographic attributes of 97 UPNM students, the study compares three data-mining algorithms in WEKA to predict pass/fail — finding gender, age & student status most influential, with the Neural Network most accurate (RMSE 0.0006) and the Decision Tree least (RMSE 5.16) — to help lecturers plan interventions.

Problem & aim

Higher-education institutions strive to improve achievement so graduates are job-ready. It is impossible to manually observe the main factors raising/lowering grades, so the study uses demographic profiling + data mining to predict performance and identify the most significant attributes. The performance indicator is CGPA; the study uses a 3.0 CGPA demographic profile and treats ≥ 2.75 CGPA = pass, < 2.75 = fail. The goal: give UPNM management, lecturers & students insight to modify the factors contributing to success (short- and long-term teaching plans).

Key concepts & definitions

Educational Data Mining (EDM)
Applying data mining to educational data to discover patterns & enhance the quality of education.
Demographic analysis
Population research on socioeconomic info — jobs, education, income, marriage, birth, mortality.
KDD

CGPA
Cumulative Grade Point Average — the overall achievement indicator across the enrolled session.
WEKA
Waikato Environment for Knowledge Analysis — the ML software used to build & evaluate the models.

Data & preprocessing

Dataset = 97 instances of students (Dept. of Science Computer, UPNM). Nine demographic attributes: age, gender, race, marital status, student status (cadet officer vs civilian), residential area (city/rural), study status, geographic location (Peninsular vs Sabah/Sarawak), and CGPA.

Preprocessing (in Excel + WEKA)

  • Cleaning — remove duplicates, replace missing values with the column average.
  • Merging — combine multiple sources for reliable access.
  • Selection & transformation — convert demographics to numeric codes for WEKA (e.g. gender male=100/female=200; CGPA <2.75 = 10, >2.75 = 20; study status pass=1001, fail=1002).

The three data-mining methods

MethodHow it works here
Neural Network (MLP)Layered nodes with weights & thresholds, trained by adjusting weights to minimise error; used 2 and 4 hidden-layer variants with 10-fold cross-validation. Excellent function approximator; can overfit; needs maths/stats to tune.
Decision Tree (REPTree)Tree-structured classifier: internal nodes = attributes, branches = rules, leaves = result; test split 20%. Root node = CGPA; tree size 11; key attributes race, gender, age, residential area.
k-Nearest Neighbour (k-NN)Classifies by majority class of the k = 3 nearest neighbours (Euclidean distance chosen over Hamming/Manhattan/Minkowski). Low k avoids being swamped, but risks overfitting/outlier sensitivity.

Accuracy was measured by regression-style error metrics — MAE, MSE and especially RMSE (lower = more accurate; the key fit criterion since prediction is the objective).

The Neural Network classified most of the influential demographic attributes accurately (gender, age, student status exceeded the node threshold values). The Decision Tree had the highest RMSE because it did not detect all attributes — it only predicted a few demographics and classified selected datatypes at the leaves → lower accuracy.

Demographic findings (who passes?)

  • Most influential attributes: gender, age, student status (they exceeded each node's threshold).
  • Students who passed tend to be male, ≤ 21–26 years old, single, and from Peninsular Malaysia; more civilians failed than cadet officers.
  • The 4 students who failed were from rural areas; 3 from urban; most passers were Malay (the largest UPNM group), with most Indian/Chinese & other-race students passing.

Conclusions & caveats

Each method classifies differently; Neural Network was most accurate (lowest RMSE), then k-NN, then Decision Tree. The predictions help UPNM modify factors contributing to success and design strategic programs to reduce late-graduation/dropout.

Watch-out (exam accuracy): the paper labels k-NN as "unsupervised" and NN/DT as supervised. In standard ML terminology k-NN is actually a supervised classifier (it uses labelled neighbours). State the paper's framing if quoting it, but know the correct classification for the exam. Also: demographic-only features + a single institution (97 students) limit generalisability, and using demographics to predict outcomes raises ethical/bias concerns (Ch.11).
