1. Core Thesis

The term "decision support system" (DSS) is not a homogeneous category.

Systems labeled DSS differ enormously in what they do and how they do it. Drawing on detailed case studies of 56 decision support systems — distilled from a structured analysis of 8 in-depth cases — Alter builds a taxonomy of seven distinct DSS types to give the emerging field a shared vocabulary and a practical framework.

The motivating puzzle: people kept talking about "DSS in general," but the systems in the sample had little in common — like discussing "pets in general" without distinguishing dogs, cats, piranhas, and turtles.

2. DSS vs. EDP

Business computer applications were stereotyped into two categories. The main difference lies in the orientation of the system and the manner of use.

EDP — Electronic Data ProcessingDSS — Decision Support Systems
PurposeAutomate/expedite transaction processing, recordkeeping, business reportingAid decision making and planning
EmphasisMechanical / clerical efficiencyOverall effectiveness of individuals or organizations
Manner of useReceives reports on a periodic basisUser initiates each instance of use — directly or via a staff intermediary

3. The Organizing Principle

Alter first tested — and rejected — common labeling schemes because they did not cleanly separate the sample:

  • Functional area (marketing, production, finance)
  • Decision perspective (operational / managerial control · strategic planning)
  • Problem type (structured vs. unstructured)
  • Computer technology (interactive vs. batch)
  • Modeling approach (simulation vs. optimization)

The scheme that worked classifies systems by the degree of action implication of system outputs — how directly the output determines the decision. This runs along a single dimension:

Retrieve a single data item→ Ad hoc data analysis→ Prespecified reports→ Estimate consequences→ Propose decisions→ Make decisions

4. The Seven Types of DSS

The 56 systems fell into seven types, ordered from most data-oriented to most model-oriented:

A · File Drawer Systems Data

Mechanized versions of manual filing — immediate online access to individual data items.
e.g., real-time inventory / stock-status enquiry on the shop floor.

B · Data Analysis Systems Data

Manipulate current or historical data with task-tailored or general operators.
e.g., budget-control variance analysis; financial-analysis language for investment decisions.

C · Analysis Information Systems Data

Access to a series of databases plus small models, augmenting internal data with external data.
e.g., a marketing information system forecasting sales and analyzing competitor actions.

D · Accounting Models Model

Calculate consequences of planned actions using accounting definitions (definitional relationships).
e.g., voyage-profitability estimator; sources-and-uses-of-funds budget.

E · Representational Models Model

Estimate consequences via partly non-definitional / causal relationships (e.g., simulation).
e.g., plant production simulation; market-response model relating ad spend to sales.

F · Optimization Models Model

Generate optimal solutions consistent with a set of constraints.
e.g., scheduling training classes; linear-programming production-recipe planning.

G · Suggestion Models Model

Perform mechanical work leading to a specific suggested decision for a structured task.
e.g., insurance renewal-rate calculation; automated circuit-board assembly planning.

5. Two Broad Families

The seven types collapse into a simpler — if information-losing — dichotomy (Alter's Figure 1):

Data-Oriented

Operations on data: retrieval and analysis.

  • A · File Drawer
  • B · Data Analysis
  • C · Analysis Information

Model-Oriented

Simulation, optimization, and suggestion.

  • D · Accounting (simulation)
  • E · Representational (simulation)
  • F · Optimization
  • G · Suggestion

6. Comparative Findings (Figure 2)

Systems of different types differed systematically across key characteristics:

DimensionPattern across the types
Users & the key role Data-oriented (A) and suggestion (G) systems are often run hands-on by nonmanagerial line/operational staff. In analysis and model systems (C–F), a staff analyst acts as an intermediary for the manager — so intermediary–manager communication is decisive.
Key usage problem Operational systems (A, G): user motivation and training. Data analysis (B): getting users to apply the system in novel situations. Models (E, F): users not understanding what the model means.
System initiator Data analysis & analysis information systems (B, C) were typically initiated by enterprising internal users/staff; file-drawer & accounting systems (A, D) more often pushed top-down by superiors.
Key technical problem Data-oriented systems: flexible, efficient retrieval from large data bases. Model-oriented systems: developing the model itself, since existing modeling methods were often insufficient.
Implementation challenge File-drawer/accounting (A, D): defining data & handling procedural change affecting many people. Representational/optimization (E, F): credibility and users grasping the model. Suggestion (G): agreeing a standard computation method.

7. Why the Taxonomy Is Useful

Alter argues a type-based taxonomy serves three concrete purposes:

  • 1A guideline for designing systems — sketch a candidate of each type as a possible solution to surface alternative approaches before committing to one.
  • 2A guideline for implementing systems — anticipate the type-specific risks and early warning signals rather than generic ones.
  • 3A framework for communication & research — give a shared language so "DSS" stops meaning wildly different things to different people, and so researchers expect taxonomic contingencies in their findings.
Bottom line: "Decision support system" is an umbrella term. Knowing which of the seven types you are building fundamentally changes the right design choices, the likely users, and the implementation pitfalls.

8. Key Takeaways

  • 1 DSS is not one thing — across 56 real systems, "DSS in general" proved nearly meaningless.
  • 2 The useful axis is the degree of action implication of outputs, spanning data-oriented to model-oriented.
  • 3 Seven types: File Drawer · Data Analysis · Analysis Information · Accounting · Representational · Optimization · Suggestion.
  • 4 Type predicts the users, key role, technical problem, and implementation risk — intermediaries matter most for analysis/model systems.
  • 5 The taxonomy is a practical tool for design, implementation, and communication/research, not just classification.
