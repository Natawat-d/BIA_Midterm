Key idea

Core message: Data mining is a disciplined, multi-step process (CRISP-DM) — not a crystal ball — for discovering valid, useful patterns in structured data. Its two great families: prediction (classification, regression) and discovery/grouping (clustering, association).

What is data mining?

Definition (Fayyad, 1996): "the nontrivial process of identifying valid, novel, potentially useful, and ultimately understandable patterns in data." A.k.a. knowledge discovery, pattern analysis, information harvesting. It is a blend of disciplines (statistics, ML, AI, databases, visualization). Source is often a data warehouse (not always). DM ≠ statistics: DM discovers patterns in large data, often run by an end user.

Pattern types: association · prediction · cluster (segmentation) · sequential/time-series.

Two key contrasts: classification vs regression → categorical vs numeric output. Classification vs clustering → supervised (labelled target) vs unsupervised (no target).

CRISP-DM — the standard process

1 Business Understanding→2 Data Understanding→3 Data Preparation→4 Model Building→5 Testing & Evaluation→6 Deployment
PhaseKey tasks
1 Business UnderstandingDefine objectives & success criteria; translate to a DM goal.
2 Data UnderstandingCollect, describe, explore (EDA), assess quality.
3 Data PreparationSelect, clean, construct, integrate, format — the "85% of time" step (with #2).
4 Model BuildingPick technique(s), design train/test, build & tune.
5 Testing & EvaluationAssess against the business objective; go/no-go.
6 DeploymentPut to use; monitor & maintain; loop back.

Highly iterative. Alternatives: SEMMA (SAS: Sample-Explore-Modify-Model-Assess) and KDD.

Classification — methods

The most-used DM method: supervised, learns from past labelled data, output is categorical.

Evaluation — confusion matrix & beyond

Actual PositiveActual Negative
Predicted PositiveTPFP
Predicted NegativeFNTN
  • Accuracy = (TP+TN)/all · Precision = TP/(TP+FP) · Recall/TPR = TP/(TP+FN) · F1 = 2·P·R/(P+R) (best for imbalance).
  • ROC/AUC — TPR vs FPR across thresholds; AUC 0.5 = chance, 1.0 = perfect; robust to class imbalance.
Worked example: TP=80, FP=20, FN=10, TN=90. Accuracy=170/200=85%; Precision=80/100=80%; Recall=80/90≈89%. With imbalanced classes, high accuracy can hide poor precision.

Estimation methodologies

Holdout (train ⅔ / test ⅓; NN 60/20/20); k-fold cross-validation (rotate k test folds); leave-one-out; bootstrap (sample with replacement).

Decision trees & ensembles

Divide-and-conquer: create a root, pick the best splitting attribute, branch, repeat until each leaf is one class. Three design choices: splitting, stopping, pruning (pre- vs post-, to avoid overfitting).

  • Split criteria: information gain / entropy −Σ pᵢ log₂ pᵢ (ID3, C4.5/C5); Gini impurity 1 − Σ pᵢ² (CART); χ² (CHAID).
  • Ensembles: bagging (→ random forest; reduces variance), boosting (AdaBoost/Gradient Boosting/XGBoost; reduces bias), stacking / information fusion.

Clustering (unsupervised)

Finds natural groupings; no target variable; in marketing = segmentation. Methods: k-means / k-modes, SOM & ART (neural), fuzzy c-means, EM, DBSCAN.

k-Means: (0) choose k → (1) randomly place k centroids → (2) assign each point to nearest centroid → (3) recompute centroids → repeat 2–3 until stable. Distance: Euclidean (default), Manhattan, cosine. Choosing k: the elbow method (SSE vs k) or silhouette score. Always standardise features first.

Association rule mining (market-basket)

Finds affinities — the famous "diapers & beer." A rule X → Y:

  • Support = P(X∩Y) — how often X and Y occur together.
  • Confidence = P(Y|X) — how often Y occurs given X.
  • Lift = Confidence / P(Y); >1 positive, =1 independent, <1 negative (fixes confidence's blind spot).
Example: {Laptop, Antivirus} → {Extended Service Plan} [30%, 70%] means the trio appears in 30% of baskets, and 70% of laptop+antivirus buyers also buy the plan.

Apriori (bottom-up frequent-itemset search; anti-monotone pruning below min-support), Eclat, FP-Growth (FP-tree, no candidate generation).

Overfitting & the bias–variance trade-off

  • Overfitting — memorises noise; great on training, poor on test (high variance). Fix: more data, pruning/regularisation, cross-validation, simpler model.
  • Underfitting — too simple (high bias). Fix: richer model/features.
  • Goal = generalisation: low error on unseen data — hence holdout/CV.

Application cases

Visa — predictive + in-memory analytics to cut fraud while improving service; cancer research — ANN vs Logistic Regression vs Random Forest (+ variable importance); predicting movie financial success — a 9-class classification in IBM SPSS Modeler; Miami-Dade Police — predictive policing.
