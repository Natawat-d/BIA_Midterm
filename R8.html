Characterizing Patients at Higher Cardiovascular Risk for Prescribed Stimulants: Learning from Health Records Data with Predictive Analytics and Data Mining

One-sentence takeaway: combining predictive ML (to estimate each patient's 1-year cardiovascular risk) with association rule mining (to find which comorbidities mark high-risk patients), the study quantifies the added CV risk of prescription stimulants and builds an age-specific "watch list" to guide safer prescribing.

Problem & objective

Prescription stimulants (FDA-approved for ADHD/narcolepsy) rose sharply — total dosage dispensed grew ~250% (2006–2016) while ADHD diagnoses rose only ~4%, including off-label use for weight loss/depression. Stimulants can raise systolic BP (~1–3 mmHg) and heart rate (~5 bpm), raising cardiovascular-event concern, but clinical evidence is mixed and likely heterogeneous by individual. Objective: quantify the added risk of cardiovascular events from stimulant use and characterize the patients who are more adversely affected.

Data & study cohort

  • Source: TriNetX Research Network — federated, de-identified EHR (~111M patients, 78 healthcare organisations); HIPAA-compliant, IRB-exempt.
  • Cohort: adults (≥18) diagnosed with ADHD, 2010–2020; 219,965 total, 102,138 (46.4%) on stimulants ("exposed"). Stimulant use = prescribed in ≥2 months within a 12-month period (stable, long-term use).
  • Matching: 2:1 unexposed:exposed by birth year & sex; ≥2 years of history before the index date; ≥1-year follow-up.
  • Outcome: any cardiovascular event within 1 year — myocardial infarction, cerebral infarction, arrhythmia, heart failure.
  • Predictors: stimulant use + demographics (sex, race, ethnicity, age) + comorbidities mapped to CCS (Clinical Classifications Software) → 283 meaningful categories.
  • Age groups: 18–44, 45–54, 55–64, ≥65 (age strongly dominated other factors, so models were built per group).

Step 1 — Predictive risk models

For each age group, four ML models predicted 1-year CV risk, tuned by 5-fold cross-validation on 80% of the cohort (20% held out for final validation):

  • LASSO logistic regression (linear) · Random forest (tree-based) · Gradient boost (tree-based) · Dense neural network (NN) — chosen to span model families.
  • Evaluation metrics: AUROC (discrimination), F1-score (accuracy under class imbalance), Binary Cross Entropy (calibration); decision threshold set by the Youden Index.
  • Feature importance via SHAP (Shapley Additive exPlanations).

Step 2 — "Added risk" + association rule mining

The key idea: added risk = the change in a patient's predicted CV risk when you switch them from with to without stimulant use, holding all else constant — a personalised impact of the stimulant. Patients with the top quartile (25%) of added risk = the high-risk group.

ARM then finds clinical features (single diagnoses or dyads) common among high-risk patients:

  • Support ≥ 2% (frequency); lift ≥ 1.0 (positive dependence — co-occurring comorbidity combinations).
  • Ratio of added risk > 1.0 defines a critical feature; robustness via 100 replications, keeping features selected in >50% of runs.

CV events occurred within 1 year in 2.3% (18–44), 5.1% (45–54), 9.3% (55–64), 20.0% (≥65). Prescription stimulant use ranked in the top-5 most important features (SHAP) across all age groups with a positive coefficient. ARM's critical features fell into four subgroups:

  1. Risk factors associated with CV events — hypertension, diabetes.
  2. Prior cardiovascular events (coronary atherosclerosis, cardiac dysrhythmia).
  3. Substance-use disorders.
  4. Psychological disorders — anxiety, suicide/self-harm, mood disorders.

Different ages showed different signatures: suicide & cardiac dysrhythmia (18–44), coronary atherosclerosis (45–54), acute cerebrovascular disease (≥65).

The "watch list" for prescribing

Because ARM produced >1000 critical features, the team ranked singleton critical features by their ratio of added risk and truncated when cumulative coverage reached 20% of the population — yielding a concise, per-age watch list of conditions clinicians should heed before prescribing stimulants (e.g., a 60-year-old with coronary atherosclerosis showed >4× higher predicted CV risk). The list was validated on the held-out 20% and in a sensitivity analysis (high-risk = added risk > 2× the average).

Conclusions & limitations

Integrating predictive modeling + data mining characterised patients adversely affected by prescription stimulants and produced a clinically usable watch list — a first step toward understanding the heterogeneous side effects and guiding safer prescribing.

Limitations: dosing/days-of-supply was unavailable for most prescriptions (only ~10% had valid days), so dose-response couldn't be modelled; the cohort is ADHD-only (limits generalisability); observational EHR ⇒ association ≠ causation; future work = external validation & integrating unstructured notes via LLMs.

Answer: ML models predict each patient's 1-year CV risk (AUROC 0.77–0.84, gradient boost best); "added risk" = risk change with vs without stimulant; ARM on the top-25% high-risk patients (support/lift) surfaces the comorbidity patterns → a per-age watch list.
