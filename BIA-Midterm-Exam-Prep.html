Q1 Performance Dashboards & Balanced Scorecards for AIT

Model answer — opening frame. AIT is a mission-driven, non-profit postgraduate institute, so performance is multi-dimensional and cannot be judged by finance alone. Two complementary tools handle this: the Balanced Scorecard (BSC) translates AIT's vision/strategy into balanced objectives + KPIs across several perspectives (the management system — "are we doing the right things?"), and dashboards visualise those KPIs in real time for monitoring, analysis and drill-down (the measurement & display system — "how are we doing right now?"). BSC defines what to measure; the dashboard shows it.

Why both, and how they fit together

  • Balanced Scorecard (Kaplan & Norton, 1992) — a strategic management system. It forces balance between financial and non-financial, lagging (outcome) and leading (driver) measures, linked by cause-and-effect to the strategy.
  • Dashboard — a single-screen visual display of the most important KPIs, with three layers: monitoring (at-a-glance KPIs + status), analysis (drill-down, trends, comparisons), management (link to actions/accountability). Good dashboards highlight exceptions, allow drill-down, unify sources, need little training.
  • Linkage: BSC perspectives & KPIs become the contents of the dashboard; the dashboard is the BSC made live and visual. AIT would run a cascade: a strategic dashboard for the President (institute-wide BSC), tactical dashboards for each School/Dean, operational dashboards for offices (Admissions, Finance, Research).
Public/non-profit twist (worth a mark): in a university the classic BSC is re-ordered — Mission & Stakeholders sit on top (not Financial), because finance is an enabler of the mission, not the goal. State this assumption explicitly.

R1 A Taxonomy of Decision Support Systems — Alter (1977)

Likely QAlter classifies DSS by the "degree of action implication of system outputs." Describe the seven types of DSS in their two families, from least to most action-oriented.
Model answer. From 56 systems, Alter ordered DSS by how directly the output drives the decision, giving two families. Data-oriented (3 types): (1) File-drawer systems — online access to individual data items; (2) Data-analysis systems — ad-hoc manipulation of current/historical data (e.g. variance analysis); (3) Analysis-information systems — databases + small models + external data (e.g. a marketing IS). Model-oriented (4 types): (4) Accounting models — consequences via definitional/accounting relationships ("what-if"); (5) Representational models — consequences via causal/simulation relationships; (6) Optimization models — best solution under constraints (LP); (7) Suggestion models — compute a specific suggested decision. Takeaway: "DSS" is not one thing — support ranges from retrieving facts to recommending the decision, and the type predicts the users, the technical problem, and the implementation risk.

R3 Visualization of Data Inventory using VDM & EDA — Yanto & Handayani (2025)

Likely QDescribe how Visual Data Mining was applied to the clinic's inventory problem. What are the three phases of the approach, and what did the analysis reveal?
Model answer. A skincare clinic could not interpret its inventory data, causing stock imbalance and lost sales. The authors combined Visual Data Mining (VDM) with Exploratory Data Analysis (EDA) in Tableau over three phases: (1) Project planning — justify the work and define the business questions; (2) Data preparation — choose, transform and verify the inventory records (98 products, Jan 2019–Dec 2020); (3) Data analysis — choose visualisation/mining tools, analyse stock-vs-sales behaviour, then verify and present via dashboards. Findings: of 98 products, 3 were efficient and 3 inefficient (overstocked vs fast-moving), with a peak turnover spike in Jan 2020; staff validated the dashboards, which then guided reorder/stocking decisions. It applies Ch.3/Ch.4 descriptive analytics & visualisation to a concrete inventory problem.

R4 Visual Analytic for Traffic Impact Assessment — Chan et al. (2024)

Likely QExplain how visual analytics was used for Traffic Impact Assessment. Which visualisations were applied, and how was the Level of Service determined?
Model answer. To tackle congestion, the study built a spatial + temporal visual-analytics workflow on traffic-flow data from three major intersections. After data cleaning, integration and transformation, it used heat maps, parallel-coordinate charts and bar plots to show daily/hourly volumes by lane and vehicle type, plus saturation flow rates. Level of Service (LOS) — the congestion grade — was derived from the volume-to-capacity (V/C) ratio: the higher the ratio, the worse the LOS, flagging congested junctions. Findings: clear peak vs off-peak and weekday vs weekend differences, identification of the most congested intersections, and a recommendation to layer predictive models on top of the visual analytics for proactive traffic control. Demonstrates Ch.3 visual analytics supporting a transport-planning DSS.

R5 Data Warehouse and Data Mining for Business Intelligence — Kumar et al. (2024)

Likely QDiscuss how data warehousing and data mining work together to support BI. How is data mining categorised, and in which industries is it applied?
Model answer. This review argues BI rests on two pillars: a data warehouse provides the integrated, cleansed, historical data (organised via star/snowflake schemas), and data mining extracts patterns from it for prediction and decision support. Data mining is categorised by learning type — supervised, unsupervised and semi-supervised — supporting tasks like classification, clustering, segmentation and market analysis. Industry cases: retail (customer-behaviour analysis), healthcare (disease diagnosis), and finance (fraud detection). Challenges: data quality, scalability, and privacy/ethics; future: big-data integration and ML automation. It is the cleanest bridge between Ch.4 (data warehousing) and Ch.5 (data mining).

R6 Using Data Mining to Predict Student Performance — Zahruddin et al. (2023)

Likely QDescribe how data mining was used to predict student performance from demographic attributes. Which algorithms were compared and which performed best and worst?
Model answer. At UPNM (Malaysia), the study predicted student performance from socio-demographic attributes (gender, age, marital/student status, rural/urban) using the KDD process in WEKA. It framed the task as classification and compared three algorithms — Decision Tree, Neural Network and k-Nearest Neighbour — evaluated by error (RMSE). Findings: the most influential attributes were gender, age and student status; the Neural Network performed best (lowest RMSE / best at identifying influential variables), k-NN gave the most accurate predictions (k≈44), while the Decision Tree performed worst on this data. Purpose: help the university plan teaching interventions. A direct Ch.5 application of supervised classification and model comparison in education data mining (EDM).

R7 Customer Segmentation using K-Means & DBSCAN — William & Johan (2025)

Likely QExplain how customer segmentation was carried out using K-Means and DBSCAN. Which evaluation metrics were used and which method performed better?
Model answer. For a fashion e-commerce firm ("XYZ"), the study segmented customers from purchase-transaction data following the CRISP-DM framework. Preprocessing used StandardScaler + PCA; the data was then clustered with K-Means vs DBSCAN and evaluated using two cluster-validity metrics — the Silhouette Score (higher = better separation) and the Davies–Bouldin Index (lower = better). Findings: K-Means outperformed DBSCAN (Davies–Bouldin ≈ 0.593 vs 0.92), with an optimal k = 7 clusters; the result was deployed as a dashboard (FastAPI + ReactJS) to drive targeted marketing. The canonical Ch.5 lesson: clustering = segmentation, judged by cluster-quality metrics, not accuracy (there is no labelled target).

R8 Characterizing Patients at Higher Cardiovascular Risk — Yan et al. (2025)

Likely QExplain how the study identified patients at higher cardiovascular risk from prescription stimulants. How were prediction and association-rule mining combined?
Model answer. Using electronic health records (TriNetX; ~219,965 adults with ADHD, 2010–2020, four age groups), the study addressed the cardiovascular (CV) risk of prescription stimulants via two data-mining approaches working together. First, several predictive machine-learning models estimated each patient's 1-year CV risk from stimulant use + demographics + comorbidities, reaching AUC ≈ 0.77–0.84. Second, association-rule mining characterised which patients are adversely affected, surfacing risk features (prior CV events, substance-use and psychological disorders) and building a per-age-group comorbidity "watch list." Findings: it quantifies the added CV risk and profiles high-risk subgroups to guide safer prescribing, calling for external validation. A Ch.5 example combining prediction (AUC) with association rules on real structured data.

R9 Integrated DSS for Evaluating Data-Mining Tools — Salah et al. (2025)

Likely QDiscuss how the integrated DSS supports an economic-intelligence system. Which data-mining methods and classifiers does it use, and what was the result?
Model answer. The paper builds a socioeconomic-intelligence database plus an integrated, multi-criteria DSS to retrieve economic/financial intelligence quickly and to evaluate and select among data-mining tools (considering tasks, data models, visualisation, interfaces, import/export, authorisation). Methodologically it combines association-rule mining (an optimised Apriori) with forecasting/classification, comparing classifiers such as Naïve Bayes, Logistic Regression and a C4.5 decision tree (with WEKA). Result: the proposed approach beats Logistic Regression's classification accuracy by about 2.76%, demonstrating an effective economic-intelligence DSS. It pairs with Ch.1 (DSS) and Ch.9 (how to systematically evaluate analytics tools rather than just list them).

R10 Text-Based Content Analysis on Social Media using Topic Modelling — Buana et al. (2024)

Likely QDescribe how the social-media-analytics tool helps marketers find topics for Instagram content. Which techniques are used, and how was the tool evaluated?
Model answer. Digital marketers needed help choosing topics for text-based Instagram content, but no existing tool did this. The authors built a Social Media Analytics (SMA) tool that scrapes Instagram data (text, captions, likes, comments, upload time, category) via Instascraper, uses OCR to pull text out of content images, and then runs LDA (Latent Dirichlet Allocation) topic modelling to discover the dominant topics. Evaluation: expert testing showed 93% could identify relevant topic references, and user acceptance (SUS-based) was ≈ 85%; the main weakness is that it needs some programming proficiency. A direct Ch.6 application of topic modelling on unstructured social text for marketing.

R11 Social Intelligence and Web Mining for Renewable Energy Insights — Mwafaq et al. (2025)

Likely QDescribe how social intelligence and web mining were used to derive renewable-energy insights. What major techniques were used, and how were they turned into a prediction?
Model answer. Over 12 months of social-media, forum and news data on renewable energy, the study applied web mining + NLP/ML: sentiment analysis, topic modelling, and geotagged region-specific theme analysis (e.g. economic-gain vs technological-advancement framing; complaints about misinformation, malfunctions, cost). It then built a predictive model of regional adoption — combining sentiment scores + theme prevalence + a socioeconomic index to estimate ~5-year adoption likelihood. Findings: sentiment was largely positive (especially Europe/North America), the dominant themes were economic benefit and technological advances, and large regional variations imply targeted policy and capacity-building. A Ch.6 example turning web mining + sentiment into a forward-looking policy signal.

R12 Sentiment Analysis & CEEMDAN to Learn Consumer-Group Preferences — Wang et al. (2026)

Likely QExplain how the model learns consumer-group preferences from online hotel reviews. Describe the roles of BERT, CEEMDAN, and preference disaggregation.
Model answer. The goal is to learn and interpret consumer-group preferences from unstructured, time-varying, ambiguous online reviews — done in three stages. (1) BERT (BERT-Base-Chinese, deep learning) performs fine-grained sentiment analysis of hotel reviews, with BERTopic extracting the latent criteria/topics. (2) CEEMDAN (Complete Ensemble Empirical Mode Decomposition with Adaptive Noise) decomposes the sentiment time series to separate noise from the residual trend, capturing the group-consistent sentiment signal. (3) Preference disaggregation (a UTA-type method) infers the group's classification value system — i.e. how much each criterion matters. Findings: validated on real hotel reviews, it markedly improves the interpretability of group preferences and supports evidence-based managerial decisions. The most advanced Ch.6 reading — deep-learning sentiment feeding a decision/preference model.
